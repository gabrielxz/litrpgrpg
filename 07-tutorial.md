# DEFIANCE OF THE FALL: TTRPG

## The Tutorial — "Integration Protocol"

### Design Document (v1.0)

## Design Objectives

The tutorial must accomplish six things simultaneously:

- **Introduce awe and danger.** The Multiverse is vast, ancient, indifferent, and lethal.

- **Expose players to many possible futures.** Martial, arcane, social, and survival paths must all be visible and viable before class selection at Level 10.

- **Generate real behavioral data.** The Hidden Vector Engine needs clean signal across all four axes (Force/Method, Hunger/Restraint, Will/Accord, Control/Freedom) before it can offer meaningful class options.

- **Create private decision points.** Group consensus flattens identity. The tutorial must mechanically isolate players at key moments so the Engine gets individual reads, not committee outputs.

- **Teach tone, not rules.** Players should absorb how the System feels — cold, observant, administratively precise — without being lectured on mechanics.

- **End with becoming, not completion.** Players should leave feeling "I have begun turning into someone," not "I finished onboarding."

The tutorial is not a dungeon. It is a curated gauntlet of incompatible incentives designed to force revelation through stress.

### Pacing Target

Three sessions. Session 1 covers Phases 1–3. Session 2 covers Phase 4. Session 3 covers Phases 5–6. The GM can compress to two sessions for experienced groups by tightening Phase 4.

## Phase 1: The Threshold (5 Minutes of Real Time)

**Purpose:** Sever players from Earth. Establish that the process is intentional, administered, and not interested in their comfort. Get one clean behavioral read per player before they know they're being tested.

**Narrative:** Players black out on Earth. No warning, no portal, no quest-giver. They simply stop existing in one place and begin existing somewhere else.

For approximately sixty seconds of subjective experience, they are nowhere. No body. No ground. No light. Pressure and orientation exist. Nothing else does.

Then the System speaks — not in words exactly, but in meaning that arrives pre-translated, like remembering something you never learned:

*Consciousness anchored.* *Native world: Earth. Status: Integrated.* *Integration stability: provisional.* *Tutorial rights: granted. Tutorial mercy: limited.* *Mortality: active.*

This is not a conversation. The System does not answer questions. It does not wait for acknowledgment. It processes and moves on.

**The Probe:** Before the void fractures, each player experiences one brief, private sensory event. These are not choices in any obvious sense — they are temperament probes that the System uses to seed initial vector coordinates. The GM delivers these individually (via note, text message, or brief sidebar).

Example probes (GM selects or randomizes one per player):

- A distant sound — something between a voice and a frequency — pulls at your attention from your left. It is uncomfortable but coherent. **Do you turn toward it or hold still?**

- A shard of light drifts within reach. Contact produces a sharp, hot sensation — not quite pain, not quite information. **Do you reach again or withdraw?**

- A string of symbols appears in your vision, then begins dissolving. You can feel meaning in it, but it's escaping. **Do you chase it, or let it go?**

- Nothing happens. The void is silent and you are alone. Several seconds pass. **What do you do with the silence?**

- You become aware of another presence nearby — not hostile, not safe, just *there*. **Do you reach out, retreat, or observe?**

These are not class selections. They are reads on curiosity, courage, caution, patience, pattern-seeking, altruism, and tolerance for the unknown. The Engine logs them as low-intensity (0.5) events across the relevant axes.

Then the void breaks. Light, sound, gravity, pain — all at once. They fall.

**GM Note:** This phase should be disorienting and fast. Do not let players settle in, ask clarifying questions, or negotiate with the void. The System is not their friend and this is not a safe space. Sixty seconds of real-world time in the void is about right. Two minutes maximum. If players try to "explore" the void, it ends — the System does not reward stalling.

## Phase 2: The Violent Arrival (Scattered Landing)

**Purpose:** Deliver spectacle. Create immediate survival pressure. Get isolated behavioral data before groupthink infects the signal.

**Narrative:** Players do not land together. The System drops them scattered across a 300-yard radius of the tutorial zone — a stitched collision landscape assembled from the wreckage of failed civilizations. Imagine a valley where pieces of dead worlds have been bolted together wrong: bioluminescent forest bleeding into rusted machine carcasses, a half-collapsed stone arena butted against crystalline desert, floating arcane debris suspended at impossible angles overhead, weather boundaries where rain becomes ash mid-stride.

The artificiality is obvious. This place was built. The seams are visible. That is the point — the System does not pretend this is natural. It constructed this environment to evaluate them, and it does not care if they know.

**The Isolated Micro-Encounters:** Each player wakes alone and faces an immediate situation that demands a response before they can find anyone else. These encounters are brief (2–3 minutes of play each), personal, and have no "correct" answer. The GM runs them in rapid rotation or simultaneously via written prompts.

Examples (one per player, selected to vary):

- **The Dying Scavenger:** You wake next to a small, non-human creature — insectoid, broken-limbed, clutching a faintly glowing core to its chest. It is clearly dying. The core pulses with warmth and energy. The creature's grip is weak. *(Hunger/Restraint: do they take the core? Force/Method: do they act or observe? Will/Accord: do they try to communicate?)*

- **The Rubble Trap:** You wake pinned under a slab of stone. Not crushing, but immobilizing. Something is circling nearby — you can hear it but not see it. A rusted metal bar is within arm's reach, and you can see a natural crack in the stone near your shoulder. *(Force/Method: brute force or engineering? Control/Freedom: systematic approach or desperate improvisation?)*

- **The Locked Cache:** You wake next to a humming System construct — a chest or node with three distinct physical inputs: a slot shaped for a blade, a depression that responds to heat, and a panel of symbols. You have no blade and no fire, but you have hands, rocks, and ingenuity. *(Method: puzzle engagement. Hunger: do they fixate on opening it? Freedom: do they break the rules of the puzzle?)*

- **The High Ground:** You wake on an elevated ridge overlooking the valley. You can see two other players in the distance — one struggling, one exploring. You can also see a cache of supplies at the base of a cliff below you, unguarded but in the open. You cannot reach both the supplies and either player quickly. *(Accord/Will: who do they help? Hunger/Restraint: supplies or people? Force/Method: do they shout, signal, or move silently?)*

- **The Resonance Flicker:** You wake near a cracked obelisk that is emitting pulses of energy. Each pulse makes your skin tingle and your vision sharpen. A degraded skill shard lies at its base, partially embedded in the stone. Pulling it free will probably stop the pulses. Leaving it means the pulses keep intensifying — it feels like it's building toward something. *(Hunger: grab the shard now? Restraint: wait for the buildup? Curiosity vs. caution.)*

**Volatile Artifacts:** Scattered across the landing zone are crude survival tools — not a curated armory, but scavenged debris. Nobody can inspect or claim everything before the pressure forces movement:

- A crude blade (heavy, single-edged — rewards STR)

- A bent spear (long reach, fragile — rewards DEX and positioning)

- A reactive buckler (absorbs one hit, then needs time to reset)

- A degraded ranged relic with one charge (devastating but single-use)

- Low-grade armor scraps (heavy, slows movement, protects well)

- 2–3 unstable skill shards (produce unpredictable arcane effects when activated)

- A battered medkit or poultice equivalent

- A sensory tool (reveals hidden features within a Zone, but takes a Beat to use)

Who grabs what — and who grabs nothing because they were busy helping someone else — is itself behavioral data.

**GM Note:** The scattered landing is the single most important structural decision in this tutorial. It guarantees that every player's first 5–10 minutes are purely their own. The Engine gets clean signal. Do not let players "decide to land together" or otherwise circumvent the isolation.

## Phase 3: Convergence and the Volatile Economy

**Purpose:** Reunite the party. Test resource allocation behavior. Introduce the social dynamics that the Vector Engine tracks.

**Narrative:** Geography, sound, and instinct draw the players toward each other. Within 15–20 minutes of arrival, they've found one another (or at least most of each other) near a central landmark — a Recycling Node, a mound of detritus from a thousand dead worlds. Half-functional gear, broken constructs, scattered shards, and unidentifiable objects.

**The Scarcity Test:** The Node contains useful equipment, but not enough optimal gear for everyone. There is:

- One clearly superior weapon

- One set of decent protective gear

- Two or three useful but situational tools

- Several degraded skill shards of varying apparent potency

- One healing consumable (single use)

- Junk, mysteries, and things that might be valuable later or might be worthless

The party must divide these resources. There is no System-imposed allocation. No "roll for loot." They talk, argue, trade, defer, demand, or stay silent. The Vector Engine tracks everything:

- **Who claims aggressively** (Hunger, Force, Will)

- **Who defers to others** (Restraint, Accord)

- **Who proposes a system for distribution** (Control, Method)

- **Who ignores the pile and explores the periphery** (Freedom, curiosity)

- **Who takes the healing item** (self-preservation vs. group welfare)

This is one of the richest behavioral data points in the entire tutorial. Do not rush it. Let the table dynamics play out.

**First System Summaries:** After the initial scramble, the System issues its first formal status notices to each player. These are terse, clinical, and slightly unnerving:

*Survival duration: 14 minutes.* *Hostile engagements: 1.* *Resource acquisition: moderate.* *Behavioral integration: processing.*

The players now understand: the System is counting. It has been counting since the void.

**Orientation:** From the Node, players can see the four major sub-zones of the tutorial landscape stretching outward. The GM describes the sightlines — the dark arena to the north, the glowing forest to the east, the shattered arcane tower to the west, the partially intact structure with flickering lights to the south. Distant landmarks are visible beyond — things clearly too large, too powerful, or too far to reach. A floating citadel. A creature that blots out a section of sky. A wall of energy marking the tutorial boundary.

The world is enormous. They can access maybe 10% of what they can see. That gap is intentional.

## Phase 4: The Field of Ruins (Open Exploration)

**Purpose:** Expose players to the breadth of possible futures. Seed class affinities across martial, arcane, survival, and social paths. Create enough simultaneous incentives that the party cannot do everything together — forcing splits, specialization, and individual initiative.

**Structure:** The tutorial landscape contains four sub-zones. They can be explored in loose order, but the GM should engineer overlapping urgency — events in one zone have timers or consequences that incentivize splitting attention. A tremor in the Arcane Debris might coincide with a distress signal from the Civic Fragment. A hunting pack in the Wild Fragment might be heading toward the Martial Remnant.

The party should never feel comfortable exploring one zone while the others wait patiently. The world is moving whether they engage with it or not.

### Zone A: The Martial Remnant

**Description:** A ruined arena or military proving ground from a dead warrior civilization. Stone tiers, shattered weapon racks, sand floor stained with old blood (not human). Training constructs stand dormant along the walls — some intact, some sparking and twitching.

**What It Tests:** Direct combat proficiency. Weapon preference. Tactical positioning. Courage under structured threat. Willingness to protect others. Dominance behavior.

**Contents:**

- Varied weapons mounted on racks (some clearly superior, some trapped or cursed)

- Training constructs that activate when approached — scalable difficulty, they fight harder the longer the engagement lasts (rewarding decisive action over prolonged grinding)

- A locked armory behind a construct that requires defeating it cleanly (no kiting, no cheese — the arena's geometry punishes it)

- A viewing platform where someone could study the constructs' patterns before engaging (rewarding Method over Force)

- Carved tactical diagrams on the walls that grant a one-time +5 Clash bonus if a player spends a Beat studying them mid-fight

**Behavioral Reads:** Who charges in? Who watches first? Who claims the best weapon? Who positions to protect a less-armored ally? Who activates the hardest construct on purpose?

**Hidden Opportunity:** Behind the arena, partially buried: a sealed vault door with a Grade warning the players cannot yet read. It does not open. It does not explain itself. But it hums. This is withheld access — a thread for the future.

### Zone B: The Wild Fragment

**Description:** An unnatural forest of bioluminescent trees with root systems that visibly move. The canopy shifts. Sounds come from wrong directions. Predatory shapes flicker between the trunks — not attacking yet, but aware and tracking.

**What It Tests:** Perception. Stealth. Patience. Environmental adaptation. Tracking. Willingness to avoid fights rather than win them. Comfort with ambiguity.

**Contents:**

- Shifting terrain — paths rearrange slowly, rewarding spatial memory and observation

- A predator den with young (killing the adults is straightforward; the young are harmless but the den contains a high-value resource — Hunger/Restraint test)

- Edible flora that restores HP but causes temporary sensory distortion (risk/reward)

- A hidden trail that bypasses the entire zone — but only visible to characters who move slowly and observe rather than push forward

- A trapped creature (non-hostile, non-human) caught in root-tendrils that is slowly being digested by the forest — saving it costs time and attracts predators, but it knows things about the landscape

**Behavioral Reads:** Who scouts ahead? Who blunders through? Who notices the hidden path? Who saves the trapped creature? Who harvests from the predator den?

**Hidden Opportunity:** Deep in the forest, a resonance node pulses at a frequency that only characters with high PER can detect. Approaching it triggers a sensory vision — a flash of the wider Multiverse, overwhelming and beautiful and terrifying. Grants 1 IP toward whatever Principle Concept the player is most aligned with, based on their current vector state. The player does not choose the Concept. The System does.

### Zone C: The Arcane Debris

**Description:** A shattered tower or ritual complex — the remnants of a civilization that understood systemic architecture. Runes flicker on broken walls. Constructs lie dormant, half-assembled. The air feels textured — thick with residual energy that makes skin prickle and thoughts echo strangely.

**What It Tests:** Curiosity. POW sensitivity. Willingness to risk unknown consequences. Pattern recognition. Comfort with delayed payoff. Tolerance for instability.

**Contents:**

- Degraded skill shards embedded in crystalline matrices — pulling them free requires solving spatial puzzles or enduring energy feedback

- Inactive runes that respond to touch, voice, or proximity in unpredictable ways — some beneficial, some painful, some both

- A resonance chamber where a character can temporarily "borrow" a dead technique — they feel arcane power flow through them for one use, unstable and intoxicating, then it's gone. Not "I'm a wizard now." More like "I touched something vast and it burned."

- Broken constructs that can be partially reactivated by characters who experiment rather than fight — rewarding Method and curiosity over brute force

- A corrupted data-altar that will answer one question about the tutorial zone truthfully — but the question must be phrased precisely, and the answer comes in symbolic form that requires interpretation

- Traps that punish impatience and reward observation — the room that kills you if you rush through it is perfectly safe if you stand still for ten seconds

**Behavioral Reads:** Who experiments? Who hangs back? Who grabs shards despite the pain? Who asks the altar a selfish question vs. a strategic one? Who tries to reactivate a construct vs. smashing it for parts?

**Hidden Opportunity:** At the top of the shattered tower, a partially intact observation deck overlooks the entire tutorial valley. From here, a character can see things invisible from ground level — the geometric precision of the zone boundaries, the pattern in the terrain layout, the fact that the "random" debris is actually arranged in a spiral. This is a Method/Control read: does the player report what they see, or keep the information?

### Zone D: The Civic Fragment

**Description:** A partially intact administrative structure from a lost civilization — half-courthouse, half-command post, half-refugee shelter. Flickering lights, malfunctioning automated systems, and — critically — other beings.

These are not monsters. They are a small group of non-human Initiates from a different integrated world, dropped into this same tutorial from a different entry point. They are terrified, disorganized, and do not share a language with the players. The System has not granted auto-translation — communication requires effort, creativity, and patience.

They are holding a fortified position with resources the party needs (a pristine healing cache, a high-grade defensive artifact, critical information about the tutorial's final phase). They are also holding hostages, or prisoners, or refugees — it's unclear which, because the players can't yet understand the distinction.

**What It Tests:** Leadership. Negotiation. Emotional reading. Manipulation. Rallying. Sacrifice vs. self-interest. Group coordination under scarce resources. Willingness to use violence against non-hostile sentients for strategic advantage.

**Contents:**

- The alien Initiate group (3–5 NPCs with their own fears, hierarchy, and internal disagreements — not a monolith)

- A malfunctioning command terminal that can be partially operated to unlock doors, activate defenses, or send signals — but only by someone willing to sit with it and experiment (it's not a combat contribution, which tests whether the party values non-combat utility)

- A tribunal chamber where disputes were once formally adjudicated — the automated arbitration system still partially functions and can be invoked if someone figures out the protocol (rewarding CHA + Method players enormously)

- A locked medical bay with enough healing supplies for everyone — but the aliens are using it as their stronghold

- Scattered records in alien script that PER + curiosity-oriented players can partially decode, revealing that the alien group has lost members and is as scared as the players are

**Behavioral Reads:** This is the most vector-dense zone. Who tries to communicate? Who advocates for violence? Who proposes a trade? Who lies? Who steps forward as spokesperson without being asked? Who notices the aliens' internal hierarchy and tries to exploit it? Who notices the aliens are scared and tries to de-escalate? Who ignores the social situation entirely and tries to hack the terminal?

**Hidden Opportunity:** If the players successfully establish communication (even rudimentary), the alien group shares a critical warning: the tutorial zone is degrading. Something is coming. The final phase is not optional — it is a survival event, and cooperation dramatically improves odds. This information is only available through social engagement. Combat-only parties miss it entirely and walk into Phase 5 blind.

**Critical Design Note:** This zone is what prevents CHA from being a dump stat. If the Civic Fragment is cut or reduced to a shopkeeper interaction, the game loses its social axis entirely. This zone must be as mechanically consequential as the Martial Remnant — players who invest in the social path here should have measurable advantages in Phases 5 and 6.

### Temporal Pressure and Forced Splits

The four zones should not sit passively waiting for the party to clear them in sequence. The GM should layer simultaneous demands:

- While the party is in the Martial Remnant, a visible energy pulse from the Arcane Debris suggests something is activating on a timer

- While the party is negotiating in the Civic Fragment, predators from the Wild Fragment begin encroaching on the Node (their base camp / loot stash)

- Resonance events in different zones trigger at overlapping times — a player attuned to one zone feels a pull they must act on *now* or lose the opportunity

The goal is to make the party split — not because the GM forces it, but because the world presents simultaneous incentives that a single group cannot address. When they split, the Engine gets clean individual data. When they choose who goes where, the Engine gets priority data. When they argue about it, the Engine gets social data.

## Phase 5: The Resonance Isolation (Solo Vector Events)

**Purpose:** Generate the cleanest possible individual behavioral data by temporarily isolating each player in a private, unrecoverable decision.

**Trigger:** As the party explores the zones, the System intervenes directly. Each player, at a moment when they are somewhat separated from the group (the GM engineers this through the temporal pressure above), receives a private System notification:

*Localized compatibility event detected.* *Solo claim window: active.* *Duration: limited.*

Then they are alone — pulled into a micro-pocket of the tutorial, or simply presented with a situation that only they can see or interact with. These events last 3–5 minutes of real time each. The GM handles them via sidebar, note, or brief one-on-one.

**Design Principles:** Each resonance event should test at least two Vector axes simultaneously. There should be no objectively correct answer. The choice should be irreversible — no takebacks, no group consultation, no "I'll come back later."

**Example Resonance Events:**

**The Offering:** A System construct presents you with two objects. One is clearly a weapon — elegant, sharp, radiating controlled violence. The other is a seed of something — organic, warm, pulsing with slow potential. The construct's posture makes clear: you take one. The other ceases to exist. *(Hunger/Restraint: immediate power vs. unknown potential. Force/Method: weapon vs. tool.)*

**The Trapped Intelligence:** A dying consciousness — the last fragment of an ancient cultivator's mind — is trapped in a decaying rune matrix. It offers to imprint its final technique onto you, but the transfer will be painful, disorienting, and will leave you weakened for the rest of the tutorial. Or you can harvest the matrix for raw energy — a significant VE boost. Or you can try to free the consciousness, knowing it may simply dissipate into nothing. *(Hunger/Restraint: power extraction vs. mercy. Force/Method: direct gain vs. experimental altruism. Control/Freedom: structured harvest vs. chaotic liberation.)*

**The Mirror:** You find yourself standing before a reflective surface that does not show your body. It shows your behavior — abstracted into patterns, colors, movement. You watch yourself as the System sees you. A prompt appears: *"**Confirm orientation?**"* You instinctively understand that confirming locks in a tendency — it strengthens whatever the System has observed so far. Declining resets the window — your behavioral data blurs, giving you more flexibility but less momentum. *(Control/Freedom: do you accept what you're becoming, or resist categorization?)*

**The Other Player:** You find another player — or what appears to be another player — in distress. They are trapped, injured, or facing a threat. Helping them costs you your Solo Claim Window. The compatible artifact / power source / insight you were drawn to will expire. You can help them or claim the prize. You cannot do both. *(Will/Accord: self-interest vs. solidarity. Hunger/Restraint: opportunity cost of altruism.)*

**GM Note:** These events are the tutorial's secret weapon. Everything else generates behavioral data with some noise — group dynamics, time pressure, incomplete information. The resonance events are clean signal. The Engine should weight these at 2.0 intensity (Major) because they are isolated, high-stakes, and freely chosen.

## Phase 6: The Convergence Crisis

**Purpose:** Stress-test everything the players have become. Reward multiple forms of competence. End the tutorial with spectacle and a sense of genuine danger overcome.

**Narrative:** The tutorial zone is degrading. This was always going to happen — the stitched landscape was never stable, and now it is actively collapsing. The System announces this with clinical detachment:

*Tutorial Sector 7-Alpha: structural coherence below threshold.* *Initiating controlled dissolution.* *Transit gate activation: imminent.* *Survival is recommended but not guaranteed.*

A geometric wall of annihilation — a Reality Purge — begins sweeping across the landscape from the far edge. The zones start collapsing in sequence. The party must reach the transit gate at the center of the valley (the broken nexus / dead world-root they've seen since Phase 2) before the Purge catches them.

**The Complication:** Between them and the gate is a **Corrupted System Warden** — a massive, glitching construct that was supposed to manage the tutorial's dissolution but has malfunctioned. It is not hunting the players. It is trying to reach the gate itself, to escape through it, and it will destroy anything in its path. It is far too powerful to defeat in a straight fight at the party's current level.

**Multi-Path Resolution:** The Warden encounter is designed so that every type of competence the tutorial tested can contribute:

- **Martial competence** can slow the Warden by engaging it in terrain bottlenecks identified in the Martial Remnant. Players who studied the constructs' patterns in Zone A recognize similar movement logic.

- **Survival/environmental competence** can route the party through the collapsing landscape efficiently. Players who mapped the Wild Fragment know shortcuts. Players who noticed the spiral pattern from the Arcane Debris observation deck can predict the Purge's path.

- **Arcane competence** can exploit the Warden's glitching nature. Players who experimented with unstable skill shards or resonance nodes in Zone C can overload the Warden's systems temporarily — buying time or creating openings.

- **Social competence** pays off directly. If the players established communication with the alien Initiates in Zone D, those aliens provide critical intelligence (the Warden's weak point, the gate's activation sequence, or simply more bodies for a coordinated effort). If the players used violence in Zone D, they face the Warden alone and without forewarning.

- **Sacrifice** is an option. A player can choose to draw the Warden's attention while others reach the gate — a genuinely dangerous gambit that the Vector Engine logs as a Defining (3.0 intensity) event.

**The Gate:** When enough of the party reaches the transit nexus, the gate activates. But it does not activate all at once — it processes Initiates individually, and there is a brief, terrible moment where not everyone is through and the Purge is closing. The last player through is the closest the tutorial comes to a genuine death.

They make it. Probably. The System does not guarantee anything.

## Phase 7: First Recognition

**Purpose:** Pay off the tutorial emotionally. Show the players that the System has been watching. Make them feel changed.

**Narrative:** The players emerge on the other side of the gate into something that is clearly *real* — not a tutorial construct, but a genuine location in the Multiverse. Air that smells like living things. A sky with actual weather. Ground that was not assembled from dead worlds.

And then the System speaks again. Not the terse survival notices from the tutorial. Something more formal:

*Integration tutorial: survived.* *Initiates registered: [number].* *Behavioral integration: complete.* *Grade: F. Confirmed.*

Each player receives a private System summary — visible only to them. These summaries are not full vector readouts (the players should never see their numbers). They are oblique, pattern-based observations that are simultaneously flattering and unsettling:

Example summary fragments (GM selects based on actual behavior):

- *Repeated front-line engagement detected. Pain tolerance: above baseline. Resource prioritization: self-last.*

- *Environmental adaptation speed: notable. Threat avoidance preferred over threat elimination. Pattern fixation detected.*

- *Unstable resonance tolerance: above baseline. Experimentation under risk: persistent. Consequence aversion: low.*

- *Social dominance captured under uncertainty. Negotiation preference over coercion. Alliance instinct: strong.*

- *Opportunistic violence detected. Resource hoarding above median. Survival prioritization: absolute.*

- *Multi-vector growth remains viable. Specialization deferred. The System will continue observation.*

**Mechanical Rewards:**

- **Levels:** Players gain enough processed VE from the tutorial to reach Level 3–5 (the GM calibrates based on pacing needs). This triggers stat growth — their first experience of watching their Raw Power numbers climb.

- **Stat Increases:** Distributed by the System AI based on behavior. A player who fought constantly gets more STR/FOR. A player who experimented with shards gets more POW/PER. A player who led gets CHA. These are small increases, but they are the first evidence that the System rewards *identity*, not grinding.

- **Affinity Notices:** Vague, System-generated hints about Principle resonance. Not "you have Fire affinity." More like: *"**Conceptual resonance detected: pattern-class IMPACT. Monitoring.**"* These are breadcrumbs, not blueprints.

- **Titles or Tags:** If any player did something statistically improbable during the tutorial — survived an encounter they should have died in, solved a puzzle in an unexpected way, sacrificed meaningfully — the System awards a Hidden Achievement. These are rare, surprising, and feel *earned*.

- **One Stable Ability:** Each player receives one System-granted ability based on their tutorial behavior. This is not a class feature (class comes at 10). It is a minor, permanent, flavor-rich capability that signals the direction of their growth. Examples: a reactive dodge instinct (+5 to defensive Clash when Exposed), a sensory pulse (detect hidden features once per Consolidation), a rally shout (+5 to one ally's Clash once per encounter), an arcane flicker (produce a weak telekinetic push as a free action).

**The Stinger:** The session ends on an open hook. Something that makes the players understand the tutorial was the prologue, not the story:

- A gate opens to a landscape vast beyond anything they've seen — a continent, a floating city, a war in progress.

- A sky-object wakes — something enormous, distant, and clearly alive becomes visible for the first time.

- A System broadcast hits every Initiate simultaneously: a cold, impersonal Mandate with a deadline and consequences.

- The alien Initiates from the tutorial (if the players befriended them) are visible in the distance, heading a different direction — a thread the players can choose to follow or ignore.

The tutorial is over. The game has begun.

## GM Reference: Vector Logging Cheatsheet

During the tutorial, the GM should log behavioral events using the standard JSON structure. Below are the key decision points and their primary axis mappings:

| **Tutorial Moment** | **Primary Axis** | **Secondary Axis** | **Typical Intensity** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Void probe response | Varies | — | 0.5 (Minor) |
| Isolated micro-encounter | Varies | Varies | 1.0 (Meaningful) |
| Loot allocation at the Node | Hunger/Restraint | Will/Accord | 1.0 (Meaningful) |
| Zone selection priority | Force/Method | — | 0.5 (Minor) |
| Behavior within Martial Remnant | Force/Method | Will/Accord | 1.0 (Meaningful) |
| Behavior within Wild Fragment | Force/Method | Control/Freedom | 1.0 (Meaningful) |
| Behavior within Arcane Debris | Hunger/Restraint | Control/Freedom | 1.0 (Meaningful) |
| Behavior within Civic Fragment | Will/Accord | Force/Method | 1.0–2.0 |
| Solo Resonance Event | Varies (2 axes) | — | 2.0 (Major) |
| Warden crisis behavior | Varies | Varies | 2.0 (Major) |
| Sacrifice during gate escape | Highest-signal axis | — | 3.0 (Defining) |

**Rule of thumb:** If a player made a choice that surprised you, it's at least 1.0. If it surprised the table, it's 2.0. If it surprised the player themselves, it's 3.0.

## Design Notes: What This Tutorial Does Not Do

The tutorial intentionally avoids:

- **Explaining all futures.** Players should leave with questions, not answers. What was in the sealed vault? Why did that blade respond to one player? What was the spiral pattern? What happens to the alien Initiates? These unanswered threads make the world feel larger than the tutorial.

- **Front-loading mechanics.** The tutorial teaches through play, not exposition. Players learn Clashes by clashing, Zones by moving through them, Beats by running out of them. The GM should never stop play to explain a system — if a player asks "how does combat work," the answer is "roll d100, add your Force, I'll tell you what happens."

- **Selecting classes.** Class choice happens at Level 10, not during the tutorial. The tutorial generates the behavioral data that *informs* class offerings. Players should feel their identity forming, not see a menu.

- **Being fair.** The tutorial is not balanced. Some players will get better loot. Some will face harder encounters. Some will have resonance events that grant more power than others. This asymmetry is intentional — the System is not fair, and the sooner players internalize that, the better they'll understand the world.

- **Overstaying its welcome.** Three sessions, maximum. The tutorial proves four things: the world is huge, the System is watching, identity emerges through action, and many futures are possible. It does not need to prove anything else. Restraint makes the world feel larger.
